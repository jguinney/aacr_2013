Introduction

The 2013 AACR Annual Meeting provides a unique opportunity for biomedical researchers across a wide-array of disciplines to discover the latest in cancer research around KRAS and strengthen the community links with other researchers. Like most meetings, rudimentary tools are provided such as abstracts and ribbons to identify attendees based on the importance of their presentation at the meeting (general poster discussion, mini-symposium, oral presentation, awards, faculty). However, there is no systematic way for researchers to discover novel social connections premised on shared research interests or needs. While online social platforms like LinkedIn and ResearchGate have the potential to promote new social interactions among researchers, their shortcomings include lack of research specificity, and no opportunities for direct social interactions that a conference provides.

To facilitate a more transparent and robust description of the RAS research community, we propose the construction of a social interactome for AACR RAS researchers. Our three primary aims are :

  1. To promote discovery of fellow researchers engaged in similar research, and thereby encourage collaboration
  2. Provide a descriptive map of the types of research within the RAS community
  3. To enable the identification, transfer, and release of new (or pending) data

Subnetwork clusters

Starting with the base interactome network, we chose an edge weight threshold of 0.24 based on visual inspection of the distribution of edge weights, in order to produced a sparse network of only the most significantly connected abstracts. This sparse network was then imported into Cytoscape [ref] to perform Markov CLustering Algorithm (MCL) analysis using the clusterMaker app [ref]. MCL clustering identifies and ranks the most tightly interconnected subnetworks. The top subnetworks are provided as an optional view of the abstract interactome.

User Interface

Control Panel

The left-side panel contains a text field with auto-completion is provided for users to identify themselves or colleagues by name. Hit Enter or click Get network to load a network.

By default, a first-degree neighbor network is loaded showing n neighbors based on the users selection. Alternatively, the user can choose to see one of the prepared overview networks (see Data Analysis section above). Selecting a node in one network view will carry over to other network views, if that same author/abstract node is present. This allows the user to identify themselves (or a colleague) in each of the network views.


Network Panel

To the right of the Control Panel, is an interactive network viewer powered by Cytoscape Web [ref]. After loading a network via the Control Panel, you will see nodes representing individual abstracts. Each node is labeled with first and last author names and mouse-over action will reveal the abstract title. Click on a node to see the complete abstract details in the panel below. The connections between nodes are based on the similarity scores from the abstract text analysis described above. The network is interactive, so you can also drag nodes to rearrange the view. Right-click on a node to access the context menu where you can select to load a new network extending from the selected node.


Abstract Panel

At the bottom of the page is a static text area that displays the complete abstract details per selection in the network above.

Subnetwork clusters

These clusters represent the most interconnected groups within the sparsified matrix (i.e., >0.24 edge weight threshold). Given the interpretation of a connection above, members of a given subnetwork cluster tend to use the same terms in their abstracts more often than among all abstracts. This might be due to the researchers working in the same sub-domain, or equally due to arbitrary preferences in word usage. The statistical clusters alone do not imply relevant groups, but the tools to assess the nature or each subnetwork are available by clicking on subnetwork members and assessing abstracts one-by-one.
